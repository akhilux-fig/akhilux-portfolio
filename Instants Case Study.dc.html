Rethinking
Instants.

Instants is an ephemeral photo-sharing feature inside a leading social app — and almost nobody uses it. I dug into why, then redesigned its placement, viewing experience, and sharing flow around one principle: the user should always know what's happening, and who it's happening with.

Role
Product Designer — solo
Timeline

Platform
iOS · Android
Methods

This is an unsolicited concept study. I am not affiliated with the company; all product UI shown is my own original design, and the feature is examined generically.
TL;DR

Nobody could find it. Nobody trusted it.

Instants was buried as small cards inside the chat screen, gave no clue whose content you were viewing, and — worst of all — captured photos were sent to all followers instantly, with no confirmation and no audience control.

I moved the feature to a first-class tray on the home feed, redesigned the viewer around clear attribution and navigation, and inserted a lightweight consent step with audience selection before anything is shared.

01 — Context

What is Instants?

Instants lets you capture a quick, unedited photo that disappears after 24 hours — a "real moment" counterweight to polished feed posts. Conceptually it's strong: low-pressure, authentic sharing with the people who already follow you.

In practice, the feature ships as a stack of small cards tucked into the bottom-right of the chat screen. It competes with message threads for attention, looks like a promotional widget, and behaves like neither a story nor a post. Most users scroll straight past it — and the few who tap in rarely come back.

02 — Audit

Five ways it fails.

(01)
Buried placement

A social broadcast feature lives inside a 1-to-1 messaging surface. Users don't form the habit because the entry point contradicts the mental model — chat is where I talk to one person, not where I broadcast to everyone.

(02)
No attribution

Tapping a card opens a carousel of photos with no clear indication of whose Instants you're viewing. Content without a face attached feels like noise — and slightly creepy.

(03)
Unlearnable navigation

Moving between Instants relies on undiscoverable gestures. First-time users tap, swipe, and give up — there are no affordances, no progress indication, no way to tell where you are in the stack.

(04)
Capture = publish, silently

The moment you take a photo, it's sent to your followers. No preview, no confirmation, no undo. This violates the most basic expectation of consent — and one accidental capture is enough to make a user never open the camera again.

(05)
No audience control

Everything goes to everyone. There is no close-friends list, no per-Instant audience, no way to exclude your boss, your relatives, or that recruiter who followed you last week.

03 — Research

Testing my assumptions

A heuristic evaluation against Nielsen's 10 usability principles, then moderated sessions with 8 active users of the app (ages 21–34, daily users) attempting three tasks: find Instants, view a friend's, and post one.

Finding 1 — Discovery

0 of 8 found Instants unaided within 2 minutes. When shown its location, the most common reaction was "why is it in chat?"

Finding 2 — Attribution

6 of 8 asked some version of "whose photo is this?" within the first three viewed Instants. Two assumed the content was ads.

Finding 3 — Trust

8 of 8 were surprised captures auto-send. 5 said they would not use the camera again after learning this. Trust, once broken here, did not recover within the session.

"Wait — it already sent that? To everyone? That's horrifying."
— P6, usability session
04 — Personas

Two people to design for.

Meera, 24
The daily sharer

Posts stories most days. Loves the idea of low-pressure "real" moments but abandoned Instants after one session because she couldn't tell who saw what.

Needs
  • Effortless posting — under 10 seconds
  • Certainty about who sees each post
  • To see friends' moments without hunting
Arjun, 29
The cautious professional

Follows colleagues and clients alongside friends. Views often, posts rarely — and only when he's certain of the audience. One accidental share would be a career problem.

Needs
  • Explicit confirmation before anything is shared
  • A trusted, reusable close-friends list
  • An obvious way to undo mistakes
05 — Direction

How might we…

G1

Make Instants findable — move it where broadcast content already lives, with a clear entry point on the home feed.

G2

Attribute everything — the author's face and name are visible before, during, and after viewing.

G3

Teach navigation in one glance — visible progress, standard tap zones, no hidden gestures required.

G4

Never share without consent — a preview step with explicit audience choice before anything leaves the phone, plus a short undo window after.

06 — Exploration

Placement, then flow.

I explored three placements — inside the camera tab, merged into the stories tray, and a dedicated tray on the home feed. The dedicated tray won: it keeps Instants' "unpolished" identity distinct from stories while inheriting the feed's daily traffic.

09 — Validation

Did it work?

I retested the same three tasks with 5 new participants on a Figma prototype. All 5 completed every task unaided. Average time to find Instants dropped from "never" to under 15 seconds, and — the number I care most about — all 5 correctly predicted who would see their post before sending it.

"Oh, it asks me first. Okay, that I would actually use."
— P3, validation round

What I'd do next: test whether the consent step adds friction for power users like Meera (a "always send to close friends" default could skip it), and explore how the tray behaves at 50+ followed accounts.
